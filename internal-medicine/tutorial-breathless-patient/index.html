    Approach to the Breathless Patient¶

    ✍️ Author: Dr A. Mokoena  |  ✏️ Editor: Dr S. Pillay  |  📅 2025-11-14  |  📌 Version 2  |  APPROVED

    Learning Objectives¶

    • Identify the common causes of acute dyspnoea
    • Apply a systematic ABCDE approach to the breathless patient
    • Differentiate cardiac vs respiratory vs other causes
    • Know which investigations to order and why

    Prerequisites¶

    • Basic thoracic anatomy
    • Cardiovascular and respiratory physiology (heart sounds, breath sounds)
    • Ability to measure vital signs

    Background¶

    Dyspnoea is one of the most common presenting complaints in internal medicine. A structured approach prevents anchoring bias — the tendency to fixate on the first diagnosis that comes to mind and miss other causes.

    Don't anchor

    Dyspnoea can have multiple simultaneous causes. A patient with COPD can also have a PE. Always complete your systematic assessment.

    Step 1 — Immediate Assessment (A-B-C-D-E)¶

    Before taking a history, assess for life-threatening compromise:

    Parameter Action if abnormal
    Airway Open, suction, consider adjunct
    Breathing — RR, SpO₂, work of breathing Apply O₂ if SpO₂ <94%; call for help
    Circulation — HR, BP, perfusion IV access, fluids if shocked
    Disability — GCS Protect airway if GCS ≤8
    Exposure — look for clues Oedema, rash, cyanosis

    Step 2 — Focused History¶

    Onset and Character¶

    Feature Suggests
    Sudden onset at rest PE, pneumothorax, flash pulmonary oedema
    Gradual over hours–days Pneumonia, decompensated CCF, asthma
    Gradual over weeks–months Pleural effusion, malignancy, anaemia
    Pleuritic (worse on inspiration) PE, pleuritis, pneumothorax, pneumonia
    Orthopnoea/PND Left heart failure
    Exercise intolerance only Anaemia, deconditioning, early CCF

    Associated Symptoms¶

    • Fever, cough, purulent sputum → pneumonia
    • Haemoptysis → TB, malignancy, PE
    • Wheeze → asthma, COPD, CCF (cardiac asthma)
    • Leg swelling, calf pain → DVT/PE
    • Chest pain → ACS, PE, pneumothorax, pericarditis

    Step 3 — Examination¶

    Examine in a systematic order:

    1. General — distress, posture, cyanosis, anaemia, clubbing
    2. Hands — asterixis (CO₂ retention), nicotine staining
    3. Face — JVP (raise head of bed to 45°)
    4. Trachea — midline? Deviated away = tension pneumothorax or large effusion
    5. Chest — inspect, percuss, auscultate
    6. Legs — pitting oedema, calf tenderness

    Key Percussion and Auscultation Findings¶

    Finding Diagnosis
    Stony dull + reduced breath sounds Pleural effusion
    Hyper-resonant + reduced breath sounds Pneumothorax
    Dull + bronchial breathing Consolidation (pneumonia)
    Fine bibasal crackles + wheeze Pulmonary oedema
    Widespread wheeze Asthma / COPD

    Step 4 — Investigations¶

    Order in order of urgency:

    • SpO₂ and ABG (if SpO₂ <94% or severe distress)
    • 12-lead ECG (PE pattern, ACS, arrhythmia)
    • CXR (consolidation, effusion, pneumothorax, cardiomegaly)
    • FBC — anaemia, infection
    • U&E — renal function, electrolytes
    • BNP or proBNP — raised in CCF
    • D-dimer — if PE suspected and low-intermediate probability (Wells score)
    • Troponin — if ACS suspected
    • CT pulmonary angiogram — definitive PE diagnosis
    • Echocardiogram — cardiac function, effusion
    • Sputum culture — pneumonia workup
    • Spirometry — not in acute setting

    Summary¶

    Step Key action
    1 ABCDE — treat threats immediately, apply O₂
    2 History — onset, character, associated symptoms
    3 Examination — JVP, trachea, percussion, auscultation
    4 Investigations — ECG, CXR, ABG, FBC, BNP, D-dimer

    Test Yourself¶

    1. A 65-year-old presents with sudden dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, and HR 112. Wells score = 5. What is your next step?
    2. A patient with known CCF presents with worsening dyspnoea, orthopnoea, and bibasal crackles. What one investigation most helps confirm decompensation?
    3. A 22-year-old presents with dyspnoea and wheeze. SpO₂ 91% on air. What is your immediate management?
